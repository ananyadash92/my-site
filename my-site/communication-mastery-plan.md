## PHASE 1: FOUNDATION (Months 1-4)
### "Own the Room You're Already In"

Before you step on a stage, you master the rooms you're already in. This phase is about rewiring daily communication habits.

---

### 1A. The Voice — Your Instrument

Your voice is a physical instrument. It needs training like any other.

**Daily practice (15 minutes every morning):**

| Minute | Exercise | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 0-3 | **Diaphragmatic breathing** — inhale 4 counts, hold 4, exhale 6. Hand on belly, not chest. | All vocal power comes from breath support. Shallow breathers sound thin and rush their words. |
| 3-6 | **Humming scales** — hum up and down your range, feeling vibration in your chest, then nose, then forehead. | Expands your resonance. Most women speak in a narrow range. You need 3 registers: chest (authority), mid (conversation), head (energy). |
| 6-9 | **Pace variation drill** — read a paragraph at 3 speeds: slow (80 wpm), normal (130 wpm), fast (170 wpm). | You need to control pace consciously. Most nervous speakers default to fast. Slow = power. |
| 9-12 | **The pause drill** — read a passage and insert a deliberate 3-second pause after every sentence. Out loud. Resist the urge to fill it. | The pause is the most powerful tool in speaking. It signals confidence. It lets ideas land. Most people fear it. You must love it. |
| 12-15 | **Record and listen** — record yourself saying "Here's what I think, and here's why it matters" in 3 different ways: boardroom, coaching session, stage. | Builds self-awareness of your vocal patterns. You can't fix what you can't hear. |

**Weekly:**
- Record one 2-minute voice memo summarising your week's key insight. Listen back. Note: pace, filler words ("so," "right," "basically"), upspeak (ending statements as questions), and energy.

**Tool:** Use the Orai app or ELSA Speak for real-time feedback on pace, filler words, and clarity.

---

### 1B. Meetings — The 60-Second Authority Framework

Most people ramble in meetings. You will not. Train yourself to speak in **structured micro-interventions**.

**The S-E-A Framework (use this every time you speak in a meeting):**

| Step | What | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **S** — State | Lead with your position. One sentence. | "I think we should delay the rollout by two weeks." |
| **E** — Evidence | One proof point. Data, example, or precedent. | "The UAT results show three critical defects in the payment flow." |
| **A** — Ask | End with a clear next step or question. | "Can we align on a revised timeline by Thursday?" |

**Total time:** 30-60 seconds. That's it.

**Practice:**
- In every meeting this month, speak using S-E-A at least once.
- After the meeting, write down what you said. Grade it: Did I lead with the position? Was my evidence specific? Did I end with an ask?
- Track filler words. Ask a trusted colleague to count them for you in one meeting. The number will shock you. That's the point.

**Advanced move (Month 2):**
- **The strategic pre-frame.** Before making your point, name *why* it matters: "Before we move on, I want to flag something that has downstream impact on the Q3 timeline." This signals to the room: listen, this matters. It's a status move disguised as helpfulness.

---

### 1C. The Narrative Muscle — Daily Storytelling Practice

TEDx speakers don't tell stories because they're "nice." They tell stories because the human brain retains narrative 22x better than data.

**The 3-Story Bank — Build this over Phase 1:**

You need three types of stories, rehearsed and ready:

| Type | Purpose | Your version |
|---|---|---|
| **Origin story** | Why you do what you do | The moment inside Tata Steel when you realised transformation fails when leaders can't translate complexity. What were you seeing? What broke? What did you decide? |
| **Transformation story** | A client/team/person who changed | A leader you coached through a pivot, or a CX initiative that shifted how an org operated. Before → turning point → after. |
| **Belief story** | A core conviction, tested by experience | "CX is not about happiness. It's about reliability and trust." Where did that belief come from? What challenged it? Why does it hold? |

**Exercise — The 2-Minute Story Drill (3x per week):**
1. Set a timer for 2 minutes.
2. Tell one story out loud. To yourself. To a mirror. To your phone camera.
3. Structure: **Hook** (one surprising sentence) → **Tension** (what was at stake) → **Turn** (the insight or shift) → **Landing** (the one-line takeaway).
4. Review. Was the hook actually surprising? Did you rush the tension? Did the landing stick?

**The hook test:** If your first sentence could be said by anyone about anything, it's not a hook. "I want to talk about transformation" — not a hook. "I spent three hours preparing a presentation that my skip manager summarised in one sentence — and his version was better" — that's a hook.

---

### 1D. Body Language — The Silent 55%

Research (Mehrabian, corrected for context): in ambiguous situations, 55% of communication impact is visual, 38% vocal, 7% words. On stage, your body speaks before your mouth does.

**Awareness drills (Month 1-2):**

- **Film yourself for 5 minutes** talking about any topic. Watch on mute. What do you see? Swaying? Closed posture? Hands in pockets? Looking down? This is your baseline.
- **Power posture practice:** Before any important meeting, stand in an open stance for 2 minutes (feet shoulder-width, arms uncrossed, chin level). Not as pseudo-science — as a nervous system reset.

**Specific fixes to train:**

| Habit to build | How to train it |
|---|---|
| **Eye contact (meetings)** | Pick one person per sentence. Complete the thought while looking at them. Move to the next person for the next thought. Not scanning — connecting. |
| **Stillness** | Stand in one spot and deliver 60 seconds without shifting weight. Stillness = authority. Movement = nervous energy (unless intentional). |
| **Gesture intentionality** | Only gesture to emphasise. Rest position: hands at sides or lightly clasped in front. Every gesture should "earn its place" (your own rule, applied to your body). |
| **The walk-and-stop** | On stage, walk to a new position, then stop and deliver a point. Walk again. Stop. This creates visual punctuation. Never pace. |

## PHASE 2: VISIBILITY (Months 5-8)
### "Be Known Outside the Room You're In"

Now you take the skills from Phase 1 and put them in front of audiences — starting small, scaling up.

---

### 2A. LinkedIn Video — The 90-Second Authority Builder

This is the fastest path to external visibility for someone with your profile. One video per week.

**The HBCL Format (Hook-Bridge-Core-Land):**

| Part | Duration | What to say |
|---|---|---|
| **Hook** | 0-8 sec | A provocative statement or question. Must stop the scroll. "The most dangerous word in any performance review is 'potential.'" |
| **Bridge** | 8-15 sec | Context. Why this matters. "I've sat through hundreds of review cycles at Tata Steel, and this one word has stalled more careers than any actual performance gap." |
| **Core** | 15-60 sec | Your insight. One idea. Structured. Use the same S-E-A or story framework. |
| **Land** | 60-90 sec | The net-net. One sentence. "Potential is a compliment disguised as a delay. If they see it, they should fund it." |

**Production (keep it simple):**
- Phone camera, natural light, plain background (or your office setup)
- Look at the camera lens, not the screen
- No script visible. Bullet points on a sticky note behind the phone at most.
- Speak 10% slower than feels natural. Camera speed feels 20% faster than real life.
- First 3 seconds: your face must be animated. Dead-face openings kill videos.

**Content calendar — 4 videos per month:**

| Week | Topic type | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Hot take** — a belief that challenges conventional thinking | "CX is not about happiness." |
| 2 | **Story** — a real experience with a lesson | "The meeting that changed how I prepare for leadership reviews." |
| 3 | **Framework** — teach a mental model | "The 3-question test I use before any transformation initiative." |
| 4 | **Reflection** — philosophical, personal, human | "What 15 years inside one organisation teaches you about patience." |

**Metric to track:** Not likes. Watch-through rate. If people watch past 30 seconds, your hook is working. If they don't, iterate on the opening.

---

### 2B. Corporate Stage — Your First 3 Internal Talks

Before external stages, own the internal ones. These are lower-stakes, higher-frequency practice grounds.

**How to get on internal stages:**
1. Volunteer for knowledge-sharing sessions, brown bag lunches, town halls
2. Propose a 15-minute talk to your leadership chain: "I'd like to present a framework on [CX governance / AI adoption readiness / transformation storytelling] at the next [team offsite / quarterly review / leadership huddle]"
3. Offer to facilitate, not just present. Facilitation is a higher-status communication act than presenting.

**Talk structure — The 15-Minute Corporate Talk:**

| Segment | Duration | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| **Cold open** | 1 min | Start with a story or a provocative question. NO "thank you for having me" — that's dead air. |
| **The problem** | 3 min | Name the pain the audience feels. Use their language. "How many of you have sat through a 40-page CX report and still couldn't answer 'so what?'" |
| **Your framework** | 6 min | The structured insight. 3 pillars max. Use before/after. Use real examples from inside the org (anonymised if needed). |
| **The turn** | 3 min | The unexpected insight or reframe. "The problem was never the data. It was the narrative layer between data and decision." |
| **The ask** | 2 min | What you want them to do, think, or feel differently. End with your net-net. Full stop. No "so yeah, that's it." |

**After each talk:**
- Ask 2 trusted people: "What's the one thing you remember?" If they can't name it in one sentence, your message wasn't clear enough.
- Film it if possible. Watch it on 2x speed — your body language patterns become immediately obvious.

### 2C. Women & Wellness Events — The Vulnerability-Authority Balance

These audiences are different from corporate. They want you to be *human* first, expert second. The skill here is calibrated vulnerability.

**The rule:** Share the scar, not the wound. Talk about struggles you've *processed*, not ones you're still in. Raw pain creates discomfort. Processed pain creates connection.

**Talk structure for these events:**

| Part | What | How |
|---|---|---|
| **The moment** | Open with a specific, sensory memory | "I was standing in a conference room in Jamshedpur, the only woman in a room of 14, and someone looked past me to ask my male colleague the question I had just answered." |
| **The pattern** | Zoom out — this isn't just your story | "Every woman in a complex enterprise has a version of that moment." |
| **The insight** | What you now understand that you didn't then | "The discomfort in that room wasn't about me. It was about a system encountering something it wasn't designed for." |
| **The practice** | What you do differently now — actionable, specific | "I stopped performing comfort. I started performing clarity." |
| **The invitation** | Bring the audience in | "What's the room where you're still editing yourself?" |

**Key skill: the emotional gear shift.** In these talks, you need to move between vulnerability (soft, slow, personal) and authority (crisp, structured, professional). The audience trusts you when they see both. Practice the transition: slow your pace, lower your volume for the personal parts. Raise energy, stand taller for the insight parts.

---

### 2D. Panel Discussions — The Sound Bite Machine

Panels are not about long answers. They're about *memorable* answers. The person who wins a panel is the one the audience quotes afterwards.

**Three rules for panels:**

1. **Answer in headlines, then elaborate if asked.** "The biggest mistake in transformation? Treating it as a technology problem when it's a narrative problem." Then stop. Let the moderator or audience pull you deeper.

2. **The bridge technique.** When asked something off-topic, bridge: "That's an interesting point, and what I've seen it connect to is..." — then pivot to your expertise. Politicians do this annoyingly. You do it once, elegantly.

3. **The "Yes, and..." move.** When a co-panellist makes a point, build on it instead of competing: "Building on what [name] said — I saw exactly this at Tata Steel, and the pattern was..." This makes you look generous AND authoritative.

**Preparation for any panel:**
- Prepare 5 sound bites — one-sentence positions on your core topics. Memorise them. Not word-for-word, but the structure and the punch word.
- Prepare 2 stories that take under 90 seconds. You'll use one.
- Know your *one thing* — the single idea you want the audience to associate with you after the panel.

### 3A. Finding Your TEDx Idea

A TEDx talk is not a presentation. It's not a keynote. It's not a "here's what I know" talk.

**A TEDx talk is one idea worth spreading.** One. Not five insights. Not your career journey. One reframing of how the audience sees the world.

**The Idea Test — your TEDx idea must pass all 4:**

| Test | Question | Pass/Fail |
|---|---|---|
| **Surprise** | Does this challenge something the audience currently believes? | If they nod along from the start, it's not a TEDx idea. |
| **Universality** | Does this matter to someone outside your industry? | If only HR/CX/enterprise people care, it's a keynote, not a TEDx. |
| **Evidence** | Can you prove this with stories, data, or demonstration? | If it's just opinion, it's a LinkedIn post. |
| **One sentence** | Can you say it in one sentence? | If you need a paragraph, you have a topic, not an idea. |

**Candidate ideas from your profile (starting points to refine):**

| Idea seed | One-sentence version |
|---|---|
| Narrative > data | "The most expensive problem in enterprise isn't bad data — it's the missing story between the data and the decision." |
| The "just" violence | "The word 'just' is the smallest weapon in the English language — and it's aimed disproportionately at women." |
| Dual-track identity | "The most powerful career move isn't specialising — it's refusing to choose between the two things that define you." |
| Coaching as transformation | "Organisations don't transform. People do. And most people are waiting for someone to ask them the question they're afraid to ask themselves." |

**Exercise:** Write 10 possible TEDx titles. Share with 5 people outside your field. The one that makes them say "I'd watch that" — that's your talk.

---

### 3B. Writing the TEDx Talk

**Timeline: 8 weeks from idea to stage-ready**

| Week | Task |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Idea lock.** Choose one idea. Write it in one sentence. Test it on 10 people. |
| 2 | **Story harvest.** List every story, example, and data point that supports the idea. You need 15. You'll use 4-5. |
| 3 | **Structure draft.** Build the arc: Hook → Tension → Insight 1 → Story → Insight 2 → Turn → Landing. Write it as a spoken script (not essay). Read it out loud as you write. |
| 4 | **Kill your darlings.** Cut 30% of the draft. If a section doesn't serve the one idea, it goes. No matter how good it is. |
| 5 | **Memorise the arc, not the words.** Learn the 7-8 beats of the talk by heart. The transitions between them. The opening line and closing line word-for-word. The middle — know the ideas, improvise the language. |
| 6 | **Rehearse on your feet.** 3x per day, full talk, standing, out loud. Film every rehearsal. Watch one per day. |
| 7 | **Audience test.** Deliver to 3 different small groups (5-10 people). Get feedback: "What's the one idea? Where did you lose focus? What did you feel?" |
| 8 | **Polish.** Refine transitions. Lock the opening 30 seconds and closing 30 seconds. These are your signature — they must be flawless. |

**The TEDx talk structure in detail:**

| Beat | Duration | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **The hook** | 0:00-0:45 | A story, a question, or a statement that creates an open loop. The audience must think "where is she going with this?" |
| **The context** | 0:45-2:30 | Why this matters. The scope of the problem. Data or a pattern that establishes stakes. |
| **Insight 1** | 2:30-5:00 | The first reframe. Supported by a story. This is where you challenge the audience's current belief. |
| **The deepening** | 5:00-8:00 | Insight 2. A deeper layer. Another story or evidence. The audience starts to see the world differently. |
| **The turn** | 8:00-10:00 | The emotional peak. The most personal or surprising moment. This is where vulnerability meets authority. |
| **The landing** | 10:00-12:00 | The idea, restated with full weight. What the audience should do, think, or see differently. The final sentence should be one they remember a year later. |

### 3C. Getting on a TEDx Stage

TEDx events are independently organised. Each has its own application process.

**Action plan:**

| Step | When | How |
|---|---|---|
| **Build a speaker reel** | Month 7-8 | Compile 3-4 clips: LinkedIn videos, corporate talks, event snippets. 2-minute highlight reel. |
| **Apply to 5-8 TEDx events** | Month 8-9 | Search tedx.com for upcoming events in India. Apply to: TEDxJamshedpur, TEDxIIMRanchi, TEDxBITSPilani, TEDxNMIMS, TEDxJuhu, TEDxGateway. Regional events are easier to get into than flagship ones. |
| **Leverage your network** | Ongoing | TEDx organisers are often reachable via LinkedIn. Don't cold-pitch. Share your content consistently → get noticed → then apply. |
| **Speak at TEDx-adjacent events first** | Month 6-8 | Josh Talks, INK Conference, corporate keynotes, Rotary/Lions clubs, IIM/IIT guest lectures. Each one builds your speaker credibility and reel. |

**Your application must include:**
- Your one idea in one sentence
- Why you (your unique credibility — 15 years, 5 domains, coaching + enterprise)
- A 2-minute video pitch (not a full talk — a pitch for why this idea matters)
- Evidence of speaking ability (your reel)
