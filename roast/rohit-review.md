# Rohit Kapoor — Angel Investor Review

**Context: I evaluate personal brands and solo practices for potential investment or advisory board roles. I'm looking at this site through the lens of: is this a scalable, investable practice? Does this person have positioning clarity? Would I refer enterprise clients to her?**

**Rating: 7/10 — Strong positioning, execution gaps**

---

## Positioning Assessment

**The good:** The intersection of "CX transformation" + "leadership coaching" + "enterprise insider" is a genuinely differentiated position. Most transformation consultants are ex-Big-4 generalists. Most coaches have never operated inside a system. Ananya has lived inside one of India's most complex enterprises for 15 years and is translating that into a dual-track practice. That's a real moat.

**The concern:** The site doesn't make this moat visible enough. I had to read the entire About section to piece together why she's different. That should be in the first 10 seconds.

## What Signals Credibility

1. **Tata Steel name-drop is powerful** — but it's buried in the About section. For an Indian enterprise audience, "Tata Steel" is instant credibility. It should be visible without scrolling.

2. **The Before/After tables demonstrate methodology.** Most consultant sites describe what they do. This one shows HOW they think. That's a signal of genuine expertise, not just marketing.

3. **"Governance-ready" language.** This tells me she knows who the actual buyer is — it's not the mid-manager, it's the C-suite who needs board-presentable outputs. That's sophisticated positioning.

4. **ICF certification** is a credibility signal for coaching, but it's underplayed. It's a small stat in the hero and a mention in the About section. For coaching buyers, ICF is the buying criterion. Make it prominent.

## What Undermines Credibility

1. **Placeholder testimonials are a credibility emergency.** I cannot overstate this. For an enterprise-facing practice, fake testimonials are worse than none. An investor looking at this would assume: she either has no clients yet (red flag) or she couldn't be bothered to get real ones (bigger red flag). Either way, it signals "early stage" when the copy is trying to signal "established expert."

2. **No client logos, no named references.** Every serious consulting site has some version of "Trusted by" or at minimum named companies (even if anonymized as "a Fortune 500 manufacturer"). The complete absence of third-party validation makes this feel aspirational rather than proven.

3. **The form doesn't actually work.** I tested it — it shows "Message Sent ✓" and then... resets. No actual submission. If I were a potential client who just wrote a detailed message about my transformation challenge and it went nowhere, I'd never come back. This is a trust-destroying experience.

4. **No LinkedIn integration.** For a personal brand in the Indian enterprise space, LinkedIn IS the trust layer. Her LinkedIn profile probably has recommendations, endorsements, and a post history that would validate everything on this site. There's a footer link but it goes to "#" — a dead link. That's worse than no link.

5. **hello@ananyadas.com** — does this domain actually exist? If someone sends an email and it bounces, the entire site's credibility collapses. If it's real, great. If it's aspirational, it's a landmine.

## Scalability / Business Model Observations

- "6-session structured coaching arc" and "4-8 week scoped briefs" are good productized offerings. They signal boundaries and structure — which is what enterprise buyers want.
- The dual-track model (consulting + coaching) is strategically sound but operationally risky for a solo practitioner. Who handles discovery calls, proposals, and project management while she's in a coaching session?
- No indication of whether this is a solo practice or a firm. The "I" voice is strong but limits scalability perception.

## Would I Refer Clients to Her?

**Based on the copy: yes.** The thinking is clearly above average. The positioning is specific and defensible.

**Based on the site as it currently exists: I'd hesitate.** The fake testimonials, dead links, and non-functional form create enough doubt that I'd want to vet her separately before making a referral. And that's the problem — the site should BE the vetting, not create a need for more vetting.

## Net-Net

The underlying positioning is strong — genuinely differentiated in a crowded market. But the execution has gaps that undermine the very credibility the copy is trying to build. Fix the testimonials (remove or replace with real ones), make the form work, add the LinkedIn link, and mention Tata Steel above the fold. Then this goes from "interesting" to "referable."
